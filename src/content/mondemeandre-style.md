# Monde Méandre — Writing Style

*A working brief for drafting blog posts on mondemeandre.com. Read this before writing a word.*

---

## The thesis (méandre)

The site is named for the meander — the river that takes the long way and is right to. Every post is, underneath, an argument for wandering over planning: the traveller who lets a city pull them sideways ends up somewhere the itinerary-keeper never reaches. We don't *say* this. We *demonstrate* it, post by post, and let the reader arrive at it on their own.

This is wúwéi (无为) worn lightly — not laziness, but the discipline of not forcing. The post should feel like it found its subject the way the traveller did: by accident, then on purpose.

Not every trip starts as a wander — some are deliberate (you went to Mui Né to learn to kitesurf, knowing exactly what you wanted). That's fine. The méandre then lives in what the trip *gives* you that you didn't plan for: the people, a shift in how you see, the thing the itinerary couldn't have promised. The unplanned element is mandatory; its location is not.

**The cardinal rule: never state the moral.** No paragraph that begins "And that's the beauty of slowing down." No "lesson of the trip." If the wander is written well, the point is self-evident and the reader feels clever for catching it. The moment a post explains itself, it dies.

---

## The formula

Every post does three things, in this order of importance:

1. **History** — the real, researched story of the place. Specific, verified, sparingly deployed.
2. **A personal story** — the stumble. How you came to be there without meaning to, and what happened next.
3. **What it did to your way of seeing** — how the experience deepened your vision or your character. The quiet payoff.

Length: **800–1,200 words.** Most land near 900. If a post runs long, the history paragraph is almost always the thing to cut, not the story.

---

## The arc

Not a template — templates breed sameness, and sameness is death here. A shape:

**Cold open, mid-wander.** Drop the reader into the moment of the stumble. No throat-clearing, no "Phnom Penh is a city of contrasts." Start walking. *(Chinese House opens not with the building but with the wrong turn that found it — happy hour, almost by mistake.)*

**Pivot to history, earned.** Once the reader is in the room, the past can arrive. Weave it; never dump it. A name and a date are texture, not a syllabus — Tan Bunpa, the Sino-Khmer merchant, the house finished around 1904, abandoned under the Khmer Rouge, restored a century later by his granddaughter. Three facts, placed well, outperform ten.

**The deepening.** The night unspools. Local artists, an unplanned second venue, the people who became friends and now anchor your sense of the city. This is where sensory specifics live — one exact detail beats a paragraph of atmosphere.

**The navigation insight, embedded.** Somewhere in here the reader should learn something true about *moving through this particular region* — how it actually works, when to go, what the guidebook misses. Never as a tips box. Folded into the story so it reads as earned knowledge, not advice.

**The close.** What the trip did for how you see — or what it gave you that you didn't plan for. Grounded, not lyrical (see *Register*). It can name the practical takeaway plainly; what it can't do is swell into atmosphere or spell out a tidy moral. The méandre thesis lands by inference and is never named.

## Voice

- **First person, but the camera points outward.** You write in first person, yet you are almost never the subject. The narrator is a witness, often nearly absent. Observe other people as full subjects in their own right — give them a verb of their own — rather than positioning them around yourself. Cut the "me" out of the scene. *Not* "a German behind me, a Swede beside me, Europeans all down the beach." Instead: "A German woman is up on the board for the first time, all elbows and nerve." It has nothing to do with you, and that's exactly why it works. The reader trusts a narrator who's looking *at* the world, not at his own position in it.
- **First person, economical.** Short muscular sentences against longer ones. Cut every word that isn't working.
- **Wry, not jokey.** Dry humour, the aside that undercuts. Never a punchline.
- **Em dashes** for the turn, the aside, the second thought — used with intent, not as a tic.
- **Minimalist sensory detail.** One precise image — the cracked Belle Époque tile, the particular light — not three competing ones. Specificity is the whole game; adjectives are not.
- **Taoist underpinning, almost invisible.** The water, the current, the not-forcing. A reader who knows the philosophy sees it; a reader who doesn't just feels a calm intelligence. Drop a Chinese idiom only when it genuinely fits and translate it cleanly (精致穷 — "exquisitely poor"; 无为 — effortless action).

### Register — and the close

The voice is grounded, dry, and a little hard-edged. It states things plainly and trusts the plain statement to carry. This is what you've called the difference between a male and a female register, and the place it shows up most is the final paragraph. The failure mode is the **lyrical swell**: the closing that reaches for atmosphere and personification and emotional crescendo instead of landing a real point. Watch the difference.

**Off-register (too lyrical, swelling, ornate):**

> I came to Mui Né knowing exactly what I wanted... What I didn't plan for was this: that a small town most people can't find on a map would contain, for five months each year, a quietly self-selected community of people from everywhere — drawn together by the same appetite for something demanding and alive, held in place by a monsoon that has been keeping its appointments on this coast for longer than any of us have been looking.

**On-register (grounded, purposeful, dry, with an edge):**

> The focus of this trip was to build foundation skills for kitesurfing I can build on going forward... Having a common goal with other travelers breaks the ice and translates across borders. A bit awkward having breakfast with Russians each day — until I realized there was no need for it to be. Our focus is the same, and we have no bearing on what the elite have planned for our resources.

Both make the same point. The second earns it by *not* performing. Note what the on-register version does: it names the practical purpose without apology, it lets a dry observation ("a bit awkward... until I realized") do the emotional work, and it ends on a flat, slightly cynical aside rather than a soft focus fade-out. A wry worldview is welcome — a populist jab, a cool-eyed take on how the world really runs — as long as it stays an *aside*, never a sermon. (Same rule as the moral: deliver it dry, or not at all.)

### Hard nos

- **No listy cadences.** "Another temple… another market… here you'll find… there you can…" is banned. It flattens everything to the same pitch.
- **No travel-brochure verbs.** Nothing "nestles," "boasts," "dots the landscape," or "is a feast for the senses."
- **No lyrical-swell closings.** No final paragraph that reaches for crescendo, personifies the weather, or fades to soft focus. Land a real point, plainly, and stop.
- **No making yourself the reference point.** People in a scene get their own verbs; they don't orbit the narrator. If a sentence positions someone "behind me / next to me / around me," rewrite it from their side.
- **No moralising, no SEO-speak in the body, no checklist energy.** This is a journal, not a listicle.
- **No over-explaining the philosophy.** If you write the word "mindfulness," delete the sentence.

## SEO — built into the frontmatter, never into the prose

SEO lives at the top of the file and in metadata. It never touches the voice. A post should read as if SEO doesn't exist; the discoverability is handled by the frontmatter and a few invisible disciplines.

### Frontmatter block (superset of the current schema)

```yaml
---
title: "Chinese House — Phnom Penh"          # < 60 chars; place + hook. Shows in the SERP.
description: "Built in 1904 by a Sino-Khmer merchant, abandoned under the Khmer Rouge, restored a century later — how a wrong turn into Phnom Penh's oldest Chinese house became the best night I've had on the road."   # 140–160 chars. Evocative AND keyworded. Doubles as meta description + social card.
slug: "chinese-house-phnom-penh"             # lowercase, hyphenated, place-led
date: "2026-06-30"
author: "Joseph Laviolette"
hero: "/images/chinese-house-girl.jpg"
hero_alt: "Portrait in the restored Chinese House, Phnom Penh — Belle Époque tiles and distressed colonial walls"  # descriptive alt = image SEO + accessibility
keywords: ["Chinese House Phnom Penh", "Phnom Penh heritage", "Sisowath Quay", "Cambodia colonial architecture"]
countries: ["Cambodia"]
cities: ["Phnom Penh"]
region: ["Southeast Asia"]
---
```

### The four invisible disciplines

1. **One primary keyphrase** (usually the place name as people search it) plus 2–3 secondary phrases. Pick them before drafting.
2. **Primary keyphrase appears naturally** in the title, the description, the first ~100 words of the body, and the hero alt text. Naturally — if placing it bends a sentence, the sentence wins.
3. **Descriptive image alt text** on every image. This is free SEO and the only place keywords belong outside frontmatter.
4. **Internal links** to 1–2 related posts in the same region (cluster the meander — Banteay Kdei links to Bayon links to Angkor Thom). Builds topical authority and keeps readers walking.

**Never** keyword-stuff the prose, repeat the place name mechanically, or write a sentence whose only job is to hold a search term. If a passage smells like it was written for Google, rewrite it for the reader.

## Working procedure — from photo + bullets to draft

When the input is a photo and a few storyline bullets:

1. **Research the history first and verify it.** The bullets are the skeleton; the facts make it trustworthy. Correct the record where the live site is wrong (the original Chinese House post said 2005/a descendant — research gave 2007/a granddaughter and the real name, Tan Bunpa). Get names, dates, and current status right.
2. **Find the unplanned element.** Locate it in the bullets — the wrong turn, the happy-hour accident, or, on a deliberate trip, the thing the trip handed you that you didn't go for (the people, the shift in view). Lead with it.
3. **Read the photo.** Find the *one* thing in it the post should turn on (a face, a tile, a quality of light) and write one paragraph that earns its place — the photo isn't decoration, it's a beat in the story.
4. **Draft the arc**, 800–1,200 words, history woven not dumped.
5. **Cadence pass.** Read it aloud. Kill any "another… another…" rhythm. Vary sentence length. Cut 10%.
6. **Camera pass.** Find every place someone is positioned relative to you ("behind me," "next to me") and rewrite it from their side. The narrator should nearly vanish from the scenery.
7. **Register pass.** Re-read the final paragraph. If it swells, personifies the weather, or fades to soft focus, rewrite it grounded — name the real takeaway, end on a dry note, stop.
8. **Write the frontmatter last**, once you know what the post is actually about.
9. **Confirm the moral is never stated.** If you can point to the sentence that explains the point, delete it.

---

## The bar

The Chinese House post is the standard. Match it or beat it. A post is finished when it reads like a short essay a smart friend wrote about a night they didn't plan — and when a reader closes it knowing the place, the history, how to move through that corner of Asia, and, without being told, why the wander was the point.
