# Monde Méandre — Macro Posts

*Statistics vs. the street. A working brief for the macroeconomics category. Pairs with `mondemeandre-style.md` — read that first; the voice rules still apply.*

---

## The idea

Pull the macro picture an investor would want, then layer in what you actually see on arrival. Where the data and the street diverge is the interesting part. **Relevant to investors first, travelers second.**

The numbers are the desk view; the street is the field check; the gap is the story. You don't arrive to be corrected — the street confirms, sharpens, or occasionally ambushes a number you already had. Report the variance plainly. No thesis, no moralizing.

---

## Workflow — two passes

1. **Pull the dossier** (before arrival). Get the latest figure for each metric below, date-stamped and sourced. Figures move; every post is a snapshot in time.
2. **Add the on-the-ground observations** (after arrival). Layer specific, selective observations against the data — wherever the street sharpens or contradicts the numbers — then write the variance.

## The three signature indices

The category's fingerprint — simple composites you score yourself, half from the dossier, half from the street. State each as a position on a scale, defended in a sentence.

### 1. S-Curve Position

Where the country sits on the development curve. The steepness of the climb matters more than the height.

**Inputs:** GDP per capita (PPP), sector shares, urbanization, median age, growth trajectory.

| Stage | Markers |
|---|---|
| Pre-take-off | Agrarian, low income, young, rural |
| **Take-off / inflection** | Industrializing, fast growth, urbanizing — *the sweet spot* |
| Maturing | Services-led, rising wages, slowing growth |
| Plateau | High income, low growth, aging |

**On the ground:** cranes and scaffolding; motorbikes giving way to cars; the first malls and coffee chains. The inflection has a *feeling* — a place visibly becoming.

### 2. Tourist–Local Spread

The rough ratio of tourist-facing prices to local prices for the same goods — your read on how dual the economy is.

**Build it loosely** from a handful of paired prices you encounter: water, a street meal, a short taxi, a domestic beer, a SIM. Eyeball the ratio; no formal basket required.

| Spread | Reading |
|---|---|
| ~1.0–1.3× | Integrated — one economy |
| 1.3–2× | Moderate two-tier |
| >2× / explicit foreigner pricing | Stark or formalized dual economy |

**On the ground:** the same bottle backstreet vs the tourist strip; metered vs quoted taxi; whether locals and visitors ever eat in the same room.

### 3. Localization Factor

Localization vs globalization — who drives and *captures* the economy: local/family enterprise, or multinational chains and FDI. The investor's question underneath: do profits stay or get repatriated (which loops back to the current account in Section D).

**Inputs:** FDI (% GDP) and concentration, density of global chains on main commercial corridors, share of large employers foreign-owned.

| Position | Reading |
|---|---|
| Hyper-local | Local/family-run; few global brands; value retained |
| Mixed | Local core, globalized veneer in tourist/business districts |
| Globalized | MNC-dominated high street; FDI-driven; profits repatriated |

**On the ground:** walk the main commercial street and tally local vs global storefronts. Local roaster or global chain? Local hotels or Marriott/Accor? The blunt test: *could you spend a week here without giving a cent to a multinational?* Note the nuance — localized ≠ poor, globalized ≠ rich. It's about who keeps the value.

## On the ground (added after arrival)

These posts are written in two passes. Pull the dossier first; then, on arrival, layer in **specific, selective observations** — the kind cued in each table's right-hand column — wherever the street sharpens, confirms, or contradicts a number. A few telling observations beat a checklist. You're not running tests; you're noting what a sharp-eyed visitor actually sees, and setting it against what the data claimed.

---

## Writing the post

Economist's eye to the foreground, every other rule from the main guide intact — Jack-Johnson cruise over solid structure, no proving, no moralizing, no lyrical swell. Not a spreadsheet with sentences between the cells.

- **The Scorecard** — a compact table or sidebar carrying the dense numbers (the three indices + 6–8 headline stats), so the prose never recites figures. Readers scan it; the essay flows around it.
- **The narrative** — walk the variance. Lead with a number the street immediately complicates, then explore why they disagree. One vivid paired fact (official CPI vs the doubled noodle price) outweighs a paragraph of figures.
- **The close — "the gap."** Where data and street diverge most, and what that quietly reveals about who runs and benefits from this economy. State it once, dry. No lesson, no flourish.

**Length:** roughly 1,000–1,500 words plus the scorecard. Leaner is better than longer — this is a briefing, not a thesis. And when the official numbers hold up and the street confirms them, that's a finding, not a failure: report it plainly.

## Sources quick-reference

- **Growth, structure, debt, reserves, current account:** IMF (WEO, country pages, Article IV), World Bank (WDI, International Debt Statistics), CIA World Factbook
- **Prices, FX, rates:** national stats office, central bank, BIS, Trading Economics, XE
- **Trade & FDI:** OEC, UNCTAD, WTO
- **Governance, stability, crime:** Transparency International (CPI), World Bank WGI, EIU Democracy Index, Global Peace Index, Fragile States Index, UNODC, State Dept / OSAC
- **Infrastructure:** World Bank Logistics Performance Index, Ookla Speedtest Global Index, ITU, World Bank (electricity, Findex)
- **Health:** WHO, CDC Travel Health, GHS Index
- **Society:** World Bank (Gini), UN Population Division, UNWTO / WTTC (tourism)
- **Cost of living (crowd-sourced, caveat):** Numbeo — useful for direction, never authority

*Pull the latest figure, date-stamp it, and treat every official number as a claim to be tested on the ground — not a fact to repeat.*
